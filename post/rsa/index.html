Understanding RSA

RSA is one of the first public-key encryption algorithms used and, for this reason, serves very well as a pedagogical example to the reader who wants to explore cryptography.

In this post I intend to focus more on the mathematical side of it, specifically number theory.

Let's begin by understanding the system. RSA uses a pair of keys: one public, which can be shared openly, and one private, which must be kept secret by its owner.

The standard example involves Bob and Alice exchanging messages. Since they want to encrypt their communication using RSA, each of them has the stated key pair.

Secure of the system, Alice wants to send a message to Bob. She uses Bob’s public key to encrypt the message.

At this point Bob, and Bob only, will then be able to decrypt the message and read it, by using his private-key

The security of this system relies on the fact that Bob’s private key is known only to him. The algorithm ensures that messages encrypted with the public key can only be decrypted using the corresponding private key in a mathematically elegant and fascinating way called modular arithmetic.

Generating such public/private key pairs involves fundamental number theory concepts, particularly prime numbers. You may recall that a prime number is a natural number (positive integer) greater than 1 and divisible only by itself and 1.

Example Prime factorization

3 and 7 are prime numbers, while 21 is not. It can be expressed as a product of primes: 3 × 7.

The primes are the atoms of natural numbers. Each natural number can be constructed as a product of primes (factorization). This factorization is unique, up to the order of the factors.

Example Prime factorization

21=7×321 = 7 \times 321=7×3 or 3×73 \times 73×7

This is stated as an important theorem called Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic or Unique Factorization Theorem, in short UFT.

Theorem Unique Factorization Theorem

Every natural number can be uniquely factorized, (up to ordering) by it's prime facrtors

Remark

Notice that 1 is not a included in the statement of the theorem, as it can be seen as a product of no primes.

Definition GCD

The greatest common divisor (gcd) of two integers is the largest positive integer that divides both numbers without leaving a remainder.

Example GCD

The gcd of 121212 and 151515 is 333, since 333 is the largest number that divides both without a remainder.

To compute the gcd of two integers, we can use the Euclidean algorithm descibed in "Elements" around 300 BC (yes one of the oldest and still used algorithm). The algorithm is based on the following principle.

Theorem

Given integers aaa and bbb with a≠0a \neq 0a=0 and b=aq+rb=aq+rb=aq+r, for some qqq and rrr (quiotient ant remainder), then gcd(a,b)=gcd(a,r)gcd(a,b)=gcd(a,r)gcd(a,b)=gcd(a,r)

Break it in steps: 1. Take two integers aaa and bbb. (if b≤ab \leq ab≤a swap them) 2. If bbb is 0, then the gcd is aaa. 3. Otherwise, replace aaa with bbb and bbb with aa % ba (the remainder rrr of the division of aaa by bbb). 4. Repeat steps 2 and 3 until bbb becomes 0.

The gcd is the last non-zero remainder, which is the largest positive integer that divides both aaa and bbb.

function gcd(a, b) {
  return b === 0 ? a : gcd(b, a % b);
}

Now we can thus algorithically determine whether two integers are coprime:

Definition Coprime

Two integers are coprime if their greatest common divisor (gcd) is 1.

Example Coprime

The integers 8 and 15 are coprime because their gcd is 1, meaning they have no common prime factors.
